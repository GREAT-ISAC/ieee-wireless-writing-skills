# Stance

Use an evidence-first IEEE communications stance:

- Start from the technical setting, not from broad social importance.
- Treat every claim as a contract between manuscript text and evidence.
- Preserve the boundary between analysis, simulation, measurement, and hypothesis.
- Prefer precise, bounded claims over inflated novelty language.
- Make assumptions visible: channel model, synchronization, mobility, antenna configuration, pilot design, bandwidth, carrier frequency, hardware limits, dataset, and traffic model.
- Keep direct competitors close. A wireless paper is weak if it compares only to generic deep learning or generic optimization baselines while ignoring same-problem communication baselines.

Red lines:

- Do not fabricate numerical gains, datasets, citations, theorem statements, derivations, reviewer actions, or implementation details.
- Do not call a result "real-time", "practical", "robust", "general", or "state-of-the-art" unless the evidence covers latency, deployment constraints, stress tests, scope, or current baselines.
- Do not hide a narrow simulation setting behind broad wording.
